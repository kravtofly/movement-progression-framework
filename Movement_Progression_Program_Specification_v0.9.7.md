# Part I — Constitutional Layer

# Program Mission

To develop safe, competent movement flyers through a structured progression system that emphasizes responsibility, competency, judgment, continuous learning, and stewardship of standards.

The program exists to:

- Develop safe movement participants.
- Develop safe movement jump leaders.
- Develop competent evaluators.
- Preserve institutional knowledge.
- Maintain standards across generations of flyers.
- Reduce movement-related risk throughout the movement community.

# Foundational Philosophy

## FP-001 — Competency-Based Progression

Advancement is based on demonstrated competency rather than accumulated experience.

Jump numbers, licenses, reputation, popularity, confidence, and perceived talent may provide context but are not primary indicators of readiness.

Competency must be demonstrated.

## FP-002 — Responsibility-Based Progression

Progression represents increasing responsibility.

Skills are evidence used to demonstrate readiness for responsibility.

Progression is not primarily the accumulation of privileges.

Progression is acceptance of responsibility.

## FP-003 — Operational Certifications and Developmental Ladder

The progression system serves two purposes simultaneously.

Operationally:

The stickers communicate capability, authority, and operational privileges.

Developmentally:

The stickers represent stages of growth, judgment, responsibility, and stewardship.

The program is therefore:

- A certification ladder on the surface.
- A developmental ladder underneath.

## FP-004 — Responsibility Precedes Privilege

Privileges are earned only after demonstrating readiness to accept the responsibilities associated with those privileges.

Responsibility is the organizing principle of progression.

## FP-005 — Standards Protect People

Standards exist to:

- Protect participants.
- Promote safety.
- Preserve consistency.

Standards do not exist to create hierarchy, exclusivity, status, or barriers.

## FP-006 — Demonstrated Competency Matters More Than Potential

Candidates are evaluated based on demonstrated competency.

Evaluators do not certify:

- Potential
- Talent
- Optimism
- Future capability

Evaluators certify demonstrated competency.

# Program Culture

The following expectations apply to all participants.

These expectations are cultural values rather than pass/fail evaluation criteria.

Participants are expected to:

- Approach movement with humility.
- Remain open to learning.
- Accept coaching professionally.
- Prioritize safety over performance.
- Seek understanding before advancement.
- Contribute positively to the learning environment.
- Recognize that earning a sticker marks the beginning of a new stage of development.

## Teachability

Teachability is a cultural value.

Teachability is not an evaluation criterion.

### Rationale

Teachability is essential to growth but difficult to evaluate objectively and consistently.

Treating teachability as a pass/fail criterion creates unnecessary subjectivity.

The program encourages teachability through culture rather than enforcing it through evaluation checklists.

# Program Principles

## PP-001 — Movement Requires Intentional Planning

Movement flight is fundamentally different from static skydiving.

Movement introduces:

- Heading selection
- Separation management
- Flight planning
- Deployment planning
- Collision risk management

These risks are often invisible to inexperienced participants.

Movement-specific training and evaluation are therefore required.

## PP-002 — Responsibility Precedes Privilege

Privileges are earned only after demonstrating readiness for the associated responsibilities.

This principle applies at every level of progression.

## PP-003 — Standards Protect People

Standards exist to protect participants, not to create status or barriers.

The value of a standard should be measured by its contribution to safety, consistency, and development.

## PP-004 — Demonstrated Competency Matters More Than Potential

Progression is based on what a candidate can demonstrate today, not what they may become in the future.

## PP-005 — Institutional Knowledge Must Be Preserved

The program should preserve:

- Coaching knowledge
- Evaluation knowledge
- Safety knowledge
- Lessons learned

Critical knowledge should not exist solely in the minds of experienced coaches, evaluators, or examiners.

## PP-006 — Rationale Is As Important As Requirements

Requirements alone are insufficient.

The program should preserve:

- What the standard is.
- Why the standard exists.
- How the standard should be evaluated.

Future maintainers should be able to understand the intent behind standards.

# Responsibility Model

Every level represents an increase in responsibility.

| Level | Primary Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| Solo | Manage yourself |
| Group | Manage your position within a group |
| Lead 1 | Manage one other flyer |
| Lead 2 | Manage a small group |
| Lead 3 | Manage a larger group |
| Lead 4 | Manage a complex group |
| Evaluator | Develop flyers |
| Examiner | Develop evaluators and protect standards |

This responsibility ladder should guide future level design.

# Responsibility Transition Model

A recurring pattern exists throughout the progression system.

| Transition | Responsibility Accepted |
| --- | --- |
| Non-Solo → Solo | Responsibility for planning and conducting safe personal movement jumps |
| Solo → Group | Responsibility for safe participation within a movement group |
| Group → Leader Tier | Responsibility for the safety and management of other flyers |
| Leader Tier → EE Tier | Responsibility for the progression and development of other flyers and the protection of standards |

This model should guide future sticker design.

# DD-001 — Competency-Based Progression

### Status

Accepted

### Rationale

Jump numbers are an unreliable indicator of competence.

Two individuals with identical jump numbers may possess dramatically different levels of knowledge, judgment, skill, and risk awareness.

Advancement should therefore be based on demonstrated competency rather than accumulated experience.

### Implications

- Jump numbers are not primary advancement criteria.
- Progression speed may vary significantly between individuals.
- Evaluators play a critical role in advancement decisions.
- Demonstrated competency always takes precedence over accumulated experience.

# DD-002 — Responsibility-Based Progression

### Status

Accepted

### Rationale

The purpose of progression is to expand responsibility rather than merely unlock new flying techniques.

Skills are evidence of readiness for responsibility.

Responsibility is the organizing principle of the progression system.

### Implications

- Every level must define responsibility.
- Every level must define privileges.
- Every level must define restrictions.
- Technical skill alone is insufficient for advancement.

# DD-003 — Teachability As A Cultural Value

### Status

Accepted

### Rationale

Teachability is essential to growth but difficult to evaluate consistently and objectively.

Using teachability as a pass/fail criterion creates unnecessary subjectivity and evaluator inconsistency.

### Implications

- Teachability is reinforced culturally.
- Teachability is not part of evaluation checklists.
- Participants are encouraged to remain coachable and open to learning.

# Part II — Specification Architecture

## Purpose

This section defines the structural architecture used throughout the Movement Progression Program.

The purpose of this architecture is to:

- Promote consistency.
- Preserve institutional knowledge.
- Improve evaluation quality.
- Simplify future expansion.
- Support software implementation.
- Support AI-assisted review and education.

All future levels shall conform to the structures defined in this section.

# Level Architecture

Every level within the progression system shall contain the following sections.

## 1. Intent Statement

The Intent Statement explains:

- Why the level exists.
- What developmental stage it represents.
- What transformation is expected of the candidate.

Intent Statements communicate developmental purpose.

## 2. Responsibility

Responsibility defines the primary responsibility accepted by a candidate at that level.

Responsibility is the organizing principle of progression.

All other sections should support the accepted responsibility.

## 3. Prerequisites

Prerequisites define minimum entry requirements.

Examples may include:

- Licenses
- Certifications
- Experience requirements
- Courses
- Recommendations

Prerequisites define eligibility.

They do not guarantee readiness.

## 4. Knowledge

Knowledge requirements define concepts the candidate must understand.

Knowledge may be evaluated through:

- Discussion
- Questioning
- Scenario analysis
- Observation of decision making

Knowledge requirements should focus on understanding rather than memorization.

## 5. Skills

Skills define observable competencies.

Skills should be:

- Observable
- Demonstrable
- Evaluatable

Every skill should have a unique identifier.

## 6. Evaluation Criteria

Evaluation Criteria define the conditions under which certification may be granted.

Evaluation criteria answer:

What evidence is required to determine competency?

## 7. Privileges

Privileges define what becomes authorized after certification.

Privileges are not requirements.

Privileges are operational capabilities granted by certification.

## 8. Restrictions

Restrictions define operational boundaries.

Restrictions communicate:

- What remains unauthorized.
- What requires future progression.
- What risks remain outside the candidate’s authority.

## 9. Advancement Path

Advancement Path defines the next available progression options.

The purpose is to provide developmental continuity.

## 10. Duplication Audit

Every level shall include a duplication audit.

The duplication audit identifies:

- New competencies
- Reinforced competencies
- Expanded competencies
- Consolidation candidates

The goal is to maintain progression clarity.

# Requirement Architecture

Every requirement shall contain three layers.

## Layer 1 — Requirement

The requirement defines the observable competency.

The requirement should be concise, objective, and evaluatable.

Example:

Determine the correct heading while planning the jump.

## Layer 2 — Student Explanation

The Student Explanation answers:

- Why does this requirement exist?
- Why does it matter?
- What problem does it solve?

The purpose is education.

Student explanations preserve learning value.

## Layer 3 — Evaluator Guidance

Evaluator Guidance answers:

- What should be observed?
- What constitutes competency?
- What warning signs should be recognized?
- What common mistakes occur?

The purpose is consistency.

Evaluator guidance preserves institutional knowledge.

# Requirement Preservation Model

Requirements are not merely standards.

Requirements are containers for knowledge.

Each requirement should preserve:

- The standard itself.
- The rationale behind the standard.
- Evaluation guidance.
- Lessons learned.

The goal is to prevent knowledge loss over time.

# Evaluation Architecture

The purpose of evaluation is to determine whether a candidate has demonstrated competency sufficient to safely exercise the privileges associated with a level.

Evaluation is not intended to:

- Reward effort.
- Reward participation.
- Reward loyalty.
- Reward potential.

Evaluation exists to certify competency.

# Evaluation Outcomes

The program recognizes two outcomes.

## Ready

The candidate has demonstrated competency.

Certification may be granted.

## Not Yet

The candidate has not yet demonstrated competency.

Additional development is required.

“Not Yet” is a developmental outcome.

It is not a rejection.

# Competency Framework

Evaluators should distinguish between three levels of performance.

## Completion

The candidate performed the task.

## Competency

The candidate performed the task:

- Safely
- Correctly
- Consistently
- With control

## Ownership

The candidate demonstrates:

- Comfort
- Consistency
- Intentionality
- Normalized behavior

Ownership indicates that competency has become habitual.

# Evidence Architecture

Certification decisions should be evidence-based.

Evidence may include:

- Direct observation
- Video review
- Structured discussion
- Scenario evaluation

Evidence should be observable and defensible.

# Video Philosophy

Video is the primary evidence artifact throughout the program.

Video allows:

- Review
- Feedback
- Consistency
- Retrospective analysis

Video should be used whenever practical.

Video does not replace evaluator judgment.

Video supports evaluator judgment.

# Sign-Off Architecture

A sign-off represents a professional endorsement.

The evaluator is effectively stating:

I am willing to personally stand behind the statement that this individual can safely exercise the privileges associated with this certification.

Sign-offs should never be treated as administrative actions.

Sign-offs are professional certifications of demonstrated competency.

# Standards Preservation Principle

The purpose of the progression system is not merely to produce certifications.

The purpose is to produce competent participants while preserving the standards that make those certifications meaningful.

Every architectural decision should be evaluated against this objective.

## Skills

### Heading Skills

### SOLO-HDG-001

Determine the correct heading while planning the jump.

#### Student Explanation

Movement flight begins with planning.

Selecting the wrong heading can create separation conflicts, deployment conflicts, and collision risks.

Safe movement flight requires understanding where the group should travel relative to jump run.

#### Evaluator Guidance

The candidate should be able to independently determine the correct heading and explain the reasoning behind the decision.

The evaluator should assess understanding rather than memorized answers.

🟢 New

### SOLO-HDG-002

Identify and establish the planned heading after exit.

#### Student Explanation

Knowing the correct heading is insufficient.

The flyer must also be able to acquire that heading after exit.

#### Evaluator Guidance

The candidate should demonstrate the ability to orient correctly after exit and establish the intended line of flight.

Duplication Audit:

🟢 New

### SOLO-HDG-003

Maintain the planned heading throughout the jump.

#### Student Explanation

Maintaining heading is essential to preserving separation and predictable flight paths.

#### Evaluator Guidance

The evaluator should observe whether the candidate can remain on the intended heading throughout flight.

Minor corrections may occur.

Large heading deviations should be investigated.

Duplication Audit:

🟢 New

## Heading Skills Philosophy

Safe movement flight requires three separate competencies:

- Determine
- Establish
- Maintain

These are distinct competencies.

Failure in any area can increase collision risk and compromise separation.

This progression can be summarized as:

Plan → Acquire → Maintain

### Risk Management Skills

### SOLO-RSK-001

Recognize when a planned movement jump is no longer appropriate and modify or abandon the plan.

#### Student Explanation

Planning does not end when the jump is planned.

Conditions may change between planning and execution. A Solo flyer remains responsible for determining whether a planned movement jump is appropriate at the time of exit.

Changing or abandoning a plan is not failure. Continuing with an inappropriate plan is failure.

#### Evaluator Guidance

The candidate should demonstrate understanding of situations that may require plan modification or abandonment and appropriate use of alternate plans.

## Purpose

The EE Tier exists to protect the integrity of the Movement Progression Program and the safety of its participants.

Movement flight introduces risks that are often invisible to inexperienced jumpers.

# Evaluator

## Status

Operationally Complete

## Intent Statement

The Evaluator level represents a commitment to the development of other flyers and the protection of the standards that guide their progression.

Evaluators recognize that movement flight introduces risks that are often invisible to less experienced participants.

An Evaluator serves as both a guide and a guardrail.

The Evaluator’s role is not to accelerate progression, create barriers, reward effort, reward ambition, or reward participation.

The Evaluator’s responsibility is to determine whether a candidate has demonstrated the competency required to safely exercise the privileges associated with a certification.

When an Evaluator signs off a certification, they are making a professional endorsement.

I am willing to personally stand behind the statement that this individual can safely exercise the privileges associated with this certification.

## Responsibility

Develop people while protecting standards.

Neither responsibility may be neglected.

An Evaluator who protects standards but fails to develop people becomes a gatekeeper.

An Evaluator who develops people but fails to protect standards becomes a cheerleader.

Effective Evaluators do both.

## Evaluator Concept Clarification

The distinction between Lead 4 and Evaluator is not primarily:

- Flying skill
- Technical knowledge
- Experience
- Jump numbers

A Lead 4 may possess equivalent technical capability.

The distinguishing characteristic is acceptance of responsibility for:

- The progression of other people
- The development of other people
- The stewardship of standards

Leaders primarily manage movement jumps.

Evaluators primarily develop people while protecting standards.

A useful question is:

Does this issue meaningfully affect the candidate’s ability to safely exercise the privileges associated with this certification?

## Competency 4 — Standards Discipline

### EVAL-STD-001

Apply published standards consistently and independently of personal preference, convenience, or external pressure.

#### Student Explanation

Standards only remain meaningful if they are applied consistently.

#### Evaluator Guidance

A useful self-check is:

Am I applying the published standard, or my personal version of the standard?

Evaluators help candidates reach the standard rather than moving the standard to reach the candidate.

## Competency 5 — Development

### EVAL-DEV-001

Support candidate growth through clear feedback, meaningful guidance, and actionable developmental direction.

#### Student Explanation

Even when a candidate receives a Not Yet outcome, they should leave with greater clarity about how to improve.

#### Evaluator Guidance

A Not Yet outcome should create clarity rather than confusion.

## Competency 6 — Stewardship

### EVAL-STW-001

Protect the integrity of the progression system by preserving standards, consistency, and institutional knowledge.

#### Student Explanation

Evaluators are responsible for more than individual certification decisions.

#### Evaluator Guidance

A useful self-check is:

If every Evaluator made this decision the way I am making it now, what would happen to the standard over time?

# Evaluation Philosophy

## Core Evaluation Question

Did the candidate satisfy the requirements shown in this evidence in a way that demonstrates competency sufficient for this certification?

Everything else is secondary.

## Evidence-Based Evaluation

Certification decisions should be based on observable evidence.

Evaluators should avoid basing decisions primarily on:

- Reputation
- Popularity
- Jump numbers
- Confidence
- Potential
- Personal relationships

Evidence may include:

- Direct observation
- Video review
- Structured discussion
- Scenario evaluation

Video is the preferred evidence artifact whenever practical.

## Competency Versus Completion

Evaluators must distinguish between:

### Completion

The candidate performed the task.

### Competency

The candidate performed the task:

- Safely
- Correctly
- Consistently
- With control

### Ownership

The candidate demonstrates:

- Comfort
- Consistency
- Intentionality

The behavior appears normal rather than exceptional.

Ownership represents deeper integration of the competency.

## Evaluation Outcomes

The program recognizes two outcomes.

### Ready

Competency demonstrated.

### Not Yet

Competency not yet demonstrated.

Not Yet is developmental.

Not Yet is not rejection.

# Observation Domain

Purpose:

Observe accurately.

Observation allows the Evaluator to:

- Identify risk
- Assess competency
- Guide development

Accurate observation protects both standards and people.

### EVAL-OBS-002

Identify behaviors, decisions, and conditions that create unnecessary risk for the candidate or others.

### EVAL-OBS-003

Accurately identify relevant candidate actions, decisions, and outcomes during direct observation or video review.

### EVAL-OBS-004

Distinguish relevant observations from irrelevant observations.

Primary Failure:

Failing to recognize dangerous behavior that places the candidate or others at risk.

# Competency Recognition Domain

Purpose:

Recognize demonstrated competency accurately and consistently.

## Core Principle

Evaluators certify competency, not perfection.

Evaluators certify demonstrated competency, not future potential.

### EVAL-CMP-001

Recognize competency rather than perfection.

Distinguish minor imperfections from meaningful deficiencies.

### EVAL-CMP-002

Recognize skill ownership versus isolated success.

Distinguish between:

- A candidate who owns the skill
- A candidate who performed successfully once

### EVAL-CMP-003

Evaluate demonstrated competency rather than assumed competency.

Base decisions on observable evidence rather than:

- Reputation
- Experience
- Jump numbers
- Expectations
- Potential

Primary Failures:

- Confusing competency with perfection
- Certifying future potential
- Certifying reputation
- Certifying isolated success

# Standards Application Domain

Purpose:

Apply published standards consistently, objectively, and professionally.

### EVAL-STD-002

Compare observable evidence directly against published evaluation criteria.

### EVAL-STD-003

Apply the same standard to all candidates.

### EVAL-STD-004

Distinguish deficiencies from minor imperfections.

### EVAL-STD-005

Avoid introducing requirements that do not exist within the published standard.

### EVAL-STD-006

Avoid ignoring requirements that do exist within the published standard.

Primary Failures:

- Evaluating reputation instead of evidence
- Creating personal standards
- Lowering standards for preferred candidates
- Raising standards for disfavored candidates
- Allowing evaluator drift

# Sign-Off Decisions Domain

Purpose:

Make responsible certification decisions based upon demonstrated competency and observable evidence.

### EVAL-SGN-001

Determine whether available evidence is sufficient to support certification.

### EVAL-SGN-002

Issue Ready outcomes only when competency has been clearly demonstrated.

### EVAL-SGN-003

Issue Not Yet outcomes when competency has not yet been clearly demonstrated.

### EVAL-SGN-004

Separate demonstrated competency from future potential.

### EVAL-SGN-005

Accept responsibility for certification decisions.

Primary Failures:

- Certifying potential
- Certifying reputation
- Certifying effort
- Certifying insufficient evidence
- Avoiding difficult decisions

# Coaching Domain

Purpose:

Support candidate development while preserving standards.

### EVAL-COA-001

Provide clear and actionable developmental feedback.

### EVAL-COA-002

Explain deficiencies in a way that promotes understanding and improvement.

### EVAL-COA-003

Identify developmental priorities.

### EVAL-COA-004

Communicate expectations clearly.

### EVAL-COA-005

Support growth without lowering standards.

Primary Failures:

- Providing unclear feedback
- Lowering standards
- Failing to explain deficiencies
- Creating confusion about expectations

# Evaluation Criteria

- Objectivity

- Judgment
- Standards Discipline
- Development
- Stewardship

Certification additionally requires:

- Consistent application of published standards
- Responsible sign-off decision making
- Effective developmental communication
- Professional conduct
- Examiner approval

Certification decisions should be evidence-based.

# Privileges

An Evaluator may:

- Evaluate candidates against published standards
- Issue Ready and Not Yet outcomes
- Certify competency within authorized levels
- Sign off progression requirements within authorized levels
- Provide official developmental feedback

- Escalate standards questions to Examiners

# Restrictions

- Modify published standards
- Create new certification requirements
- Grant authority outside authorized levels
- Certify competency without sufficient evidence
- Certify potential in place of demonstrated competency
- Override Examiner authority
- Unilaterally resolve unresolved standards disputes

# Part V — Terminology, Governance, and Change Control

## Purpose

The purpose of this section is to:

- Establish common language.
- Reduce ambiguity.
- Promote consistency.
- Define how the specification evolves over time.

This section should be treated as normative.

Unless explicitly stated otherwise, terminology defined here carries the meaning assigned within this section.

# Terminology

## Advancement

Advancement is the successful progression from one certification level to another.

Advancement represents acceptance of additional responsibility.

Advancement is not the accumulation of privileges alone.

## Authority

Authority is permission granted by the progression system to exercise specific responsibilities or privileges.

Authority must be explicitly granted.

Authority is never assumed.

Authority is never implied solely by:

- Experience
- Jump numbers
- Reputation
- Technical skill

## Candidate

A candidate is an individual currently being evaluated for certification.

## Certification

Certification is formal recognition that a candidate has demonstrated the competency required to safely exercise the privileges associated with a level.

Certification is granted through sign-off by authorized evaluators.

## Competency

Competency is the demonstrated ability to perform a task:

- Safely
- Correctly
- Consistently
- With appropriate judgment and control

Competency is the primary advancement standard throughout the program.

## Completion

Completion means a task was performed.

Completion alone does not establish competency.

## Development

Development is the process through which an individual increases competency, judgment, responsibility, and capability.

The progression system exists to support development.

Certification exists to recognize development.

## Evaluator

An Evaluator is an individual authorized to certify competency and support participant development while protecting standards.

## Evidence

Evidence is observable information used to support certification decisions.

Evidence may include:

- Direct observation
- Video review
- Structured discussion
- Scenario evaluation

## Examiner

An Examiner is an individual responsible for developing evaluators and protecting standards at the program level.

## Intent Statement

An Intent Statement explains:

- Why a level exists.
- What developmental stage it represents.
- What transformation is expected.

Intent Statements communicate developmental purpose.

## Level

A level is a defined stage within the progression system.

Examples include:

- Solo
- Group
- Lead 1
- Evaluator

Level and Certification may be used interchangeably when context permits.

## Not Yet

Not Yet is an evaluation outcome indicating that competency has not yet been demonstrated.

Not Yet is developmental.

Not Yet is not punitive.

Not Yet is not rejection.

## Ownership

Ownership is the stage beyond competency.

Ownership exists when a behavior appears:

- Comfortable
- Consistent
- Intentional
- Normalized

Ownership indicates deep integration of the competency.

## Privilege

A privilege is an operational capability authorized after certification.

Privileges are earned through demonstrated readiness.

## Requirement

A requirement is an observable competency, knowledge area, or responsibility that must be demonstrated for certification.

## Restriction

A restriction defines an operational boundary that remains in place after certification.

Restrictions communicate what remains unauthorized.

## Responsibility

Responsibility is the primary obligation accepted at a given level.

Responsibility is the organizing principle of progression.

## Sign-Off

A sign-off is a professional endorsement by an authorized evaluator.

A sign-off represents certification of demonstrated competency.

A sign-off is not an administrative action.

## Skill

A skill is an observable competency that can be demonstrated and evaluated.

## Standard

A standard is an accepted expectation used to guide evaluation and certification.

Standards exist to protect people and preserve consistency.

## Stewardship

Stewardship is the responsibility to preserve and improve standards, knowledge, and participant development for future generations.

Stewardship is a foundational concept within the EE Tier.

## Sticker

Sticker is the traditional operational term used within the movement community to describe certifications.

Within this specification:

Sticker and Certification are considered equivalent unless otherwise specified.

# Governance Principles

## GP-001 — Explicit Change

Standards should change intentionally.

Accidental change is a form of drift.

All significant modifications should be documented.

## GP-002 — Rationale Preservation

Changes should preserve the rationale behind decisions.

When a standard changes, the reason for the change should be documented.

## GP-003 — Traceability

Future maintainers should be able to understand:

- What changed
- Why it changed
- When it changed

## GP-004 — Consistency Over Convenience

Consistency should generally take precedence over short-term convenience.

Exceptions should be deliberate and documented.

## GP-005 — Evolution Is Expected

The progression system is expected to evolve.

The objective is not to prevent change.

The objective is to ensure change occurs intentionally.

# Design Decision Lifecycle

Every Design Decision should exist in one of the following states.

## Draft

Under discussion.

Not yet accepted.

## Accepted

Approved and incorporated into the specification.

## Accepted (Provisional)

Approved but subject to future review.

Used when additional experience is needed before permanent adoption.

## Superseded

Replaced by a newer decision.

Retained for historical context.

## Rejected

Considered and intentionally not adopted.

Retained to prevent future rediscovery.

# Design Decision Management

When creating a Design Decision, the following fields should be documented:

- Decision ID
- Title
- Status
- Date
- Rationale
- Implications

Optional fields:

- Alternatives Considered
- Risks
- Future Review Notes

# Specification Change Process

Major changes should generally follow the sequence:

- Identify issue or opportunity.
- Discuss alternatives.
- Create or modify Design Decision.
- Evaluate implications.
- Update specification.
- Update derivative materials.

The specification remains the master source of truth.

Derivative materials should not independently redefine standards.

Version numbers represent specification maturity.

They do not imply program completion.

Examples:

Major structural changes become less common.

Substantial changes to program structure or philosophy.
